Biological Psychology & Neuroscience

§ I
1.
Damage to which brain structure would most likely produce severe anterograde amnesia while leaving procedural memory relatively intact?
C. The hippocampus is critical for consolidating new declarative memories. Patient H.M.'s bilateral hippocampectomy produced profound anterograde amnesia but spared procedural learning, which depends on cerebellum and basal ganglia.
2.
Which neurotransmitter is most strongly implicated in the reward pathways of the mesolimbic system?
B. Dopaminergic projections from the ventral tegmental area to the nucleus accumbens constitute the core of the mesolimbic reward pathway, implicated in motivation, reinforcement, and addiction.
3.
The resting membrane potential of a typical neuron is approximately:
D. Resting potential is roughly −70 mV, maintained by the Na+/K+ pump and differential ion permeability. Threshold is ~−55 mV.
4.
A patient with damage to Broca's area would most likely exhibit:
A. Broca's aphasia produces halting, nonfluent, telegraphic speech with comprehension largely intact. Option B describes Wernicke's aphasia.
5.
Which division of the autonomic nervous system is primarily responsible for the 'rest and digest' response?
C. The parasympathetic branch conserves energy, slows heart rate, and promotes digestion. The sympathetic branch mediates 'fight-or-flight.'
6.
The trichromatic theory of color vision holds that color perception depends on three types of:
B. Young-Helmholtz trichromatic theory posits three cone types tuned to short, medium, and long wavelengths. Hering's opponent-process theory complements it downstream.
7.
REM sleep is characterized by all of the following EXCEPT:
E. Delta waves predominate in slow-wave (NREM Stage 3) sleep, not REM. REM resembles waking EEG despite behavioral unresponsiveness — hence 'paradoxical sleep.'
8.
Long-term potentiation (LTP) is most commonly associated with which receptor?
A. LTP in hippocampal synapses depends on NMDA receptor activation, allowing Ca²⁺ influx that triggers downstream synaptic strengthening.
9.
A split-brain patient who views an object only in the left visual field would most likely be able to:
D. Left visual field projects to right hemisphere, which controls the left hand. The right hemisphere typically cannot produce speech but can direct nonverbal identification.
10.
The myelin sheath that insulates axons in the central nervous system is produced by:
D. Oligodendrocytes myelinate CNS axons; Schwann cells perform the same function in the peripheral nervous system. Demyelination of CNS axons is the hallmark of multiple sclerosis.
11.
The absolute refractory period of a neuron refers to:
B. During the absolute refractory period, voltage-gated sodium channels are inactivated and no stimulus can produce another action potential. The relative refractory period follows, during which a stronger-than-normal stimulus can fire the neuron.
12.
Which structure is most directly involved in the regulation of circadian rhythms?
B. The suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN) of the hypothalamus is the master circadian pacemaker, entrained by retinal light input via the retinohypothalamic tract.
13.
According to the Cannon-Bard theory of emotion:
C. Cannon-Bard held that the thalamus sends signals concurrently to cortex (producing emotional experience) and viscera (producing bodily arousal). James-Lange argued arousal comes first; Schachter-Singer added cognitive appraisal.
14.
Which brain region shows consistent volume reduction in long-term untreated depression?
B. Neuroimaging studies consistently show hippocampal volume reduction in chronic depression, possibly due to glucocorticoid-mediated neurotoxicity from prolonged HPA-axis activation.
15.
The drug L-DOPA is used to treat Parkinson's disease because it:
B. Dopamine itself cannot cross the blood-brain barrier, but its precursor L-DOPA can. Once across, it is decarboxylated to dopamine, partially compensating for nigrostriatal degeneration.
16.
Place theory of pitch perception is most useful for explaining perception of:
B. Place theory (Helmholtz) explains high-frequency pitch via vibration location on the basilar membrane. Low frequencies are better explained by frequency (temporal) theory, which cites firing rates matching the sound.
17.
The 'fight or flight' response is mediated primarily by release of:
B. Sympathetic activation triggers adrenal medullary release of epinephrine and norepinephrine, producing increased heart rate, blood pressure, glucose release, and bronchodilation.
18.
Phineas Gage's injury, which dramatically altered his personality, damaged primarily his:
C. The tamping-iron injury destroyed much of Gage's left prefrontal cortex. His case became foundational evidence linking frontal lobes to personality, judgment, and executive function.
19.
Phantom limb sensations are best explained by:
B. Ramachandran's research showed that cortical territory corresponding to the amputated limb is invaded by neighboring representations, producing referred sensations perceived as coming from the missing limb.
20.
Which of the following best describes an efferent neuron?
B. Efferent (motor) neurons carry commands away from the CNS to effectors. Afferent (sensory) neurons carry signals toward the CNS. Interneurons connect neurons within the CNS.
21.
Which lobe of the cerebral cortex contains the primary somatosensory cortex?
B. The primary somatosensory cortex (postcentral gyrus) lies just posterior to the central sulcus in the parietal lobe. The primary motor cortex lies just anterior in the frontal lobe.
22.
The Papez circuit, an early model of emotional processing, includes all of the following EXCEPT:
E. Papez's circuit: hippocampus → fornix → mammillary bodies → anterior thalamus → cingulate cortex → hippocampus. The cerebellum is not part of this limbic loop.
23.
Damage to the fusiform face area (FFA) produces:
B. The FFA in the ventral temporal cortex is specialized for face processing. Its damage produces prosopagnosia — the inability to recognize familiar faces despite intact object recognition.
24.
Monozygotic twins share what proportion of their genetic material?
D. Monozygotic (identical) twins develop from a single zygote and are genetically identical. Dizygotic (fraternal) twins share on average 50%, like ordinary siblings.
25.
Which of the following is a glial cell function?
C. Glial cells (astrocytes, oligodendrocytes, microglia, ependymal) support neurons through structural, metabolic, immune, and myelinating roles. Only ependymal cells produce CSF, so E is too narrow.
26.
The primary visual cortex is located in the:
B. Primary visual cortex (V1, area 17, calcarine cortex) occupies the medial occipital lobe. Projections travel dorsally ('where') to parietal and ventrally ('what') to temporal cortex.
27.
Antagonistic muscle pairs allow smooth movement through:
B. When one muscle of an antagonistic pair contracts, inhibitory interneurons suppress motor neurons serving the opposing muscle, allowing smooth joint movement.
28.
Which is the correct sequence of neural transmission across a chemical synapse?
A. The canonical sequence: presynaptic action potential → Ca²⁺ influx → vesicle fusion → neurotransmitter release into cleft → binding to postsynaptic receptors → postsynaptic potential.
29.
The 'blind spot' in human vision corresponds to:
B. The optic disc has no photoreceptors because it is where retinal ganglion cell axons converge to exit the eye as the optic nerve, producing a small blind spot in each visual field.
30.
The James-Lange theory of emotion holds that:
B. James-Lange proposed that we see a bear, run, and *then* feel afraid because of the running — i.e., physiological responses produce the conscious feeling of emotion.

Cognitive Psychology

§ II
31.
According to the Atkinson-Shiffrin model, information must pass through which stage before long-term storage?
B. The Atkinson-Shiffrin model proposes sensory → short-term → long-term memory. Rehearsal in short-term store facilitates transfer to long-term.
32.
Miller's (1956) classic paper proposed that the capacity of short-term memory is approximately:
C. Miller's 'magical number seven, plus or minus two' described the chunk limit in STM. Later work (Cowan) suggests the true limit may be closer to 4.
33.
Judging frequency by how easily examples come to mind is the:
A. The availability heuristic (Tversky & Kahneman) leads people to overestimate vivid or recent events. Representativeness concerns judgments based on resemblance.
34.
In Baddeley's working memory model, which component binds information across modalities and from long-term memory?
D. The episodic buffer (added by Baddeley in 2000) integrates material across subsystems. The central executive allocates attention but does not itself store representations.
35.
Chomsky's theory of language acquisition emphasizes:
B. Chomsky argued that the 'poverty of the stimulus' requires innate universal grammar encoded in a Language Acquisition Device, directly opposing Skinner's behaviorist account.
36.
The Stroop effect demonstrates interference between:
C. Reading is automatic; color-naming is controlled. When they conflict (word RED in blue ink), the automatic response interferes with the controlled one.
37.
Loftus and Palmer's study of eyewitness memory demonstrated that:
E. Participants shown a car-crash video estimated higher speeds when asked how fast cars were 'smashed' versus 'contacted,' establishing the misinformation effect.
38.
Which best describes a 'prototype' in categorization research?
A. In Rosch's prototype theory, categories are organized around a 'best example.' Option B is exemplar theory; C is classical theory.
39.
In Pavlovian conditioning, if a dog is conditioned to salivate to a bell and then a light is paired with the bell, producing salivation to the light, this is called:
C. Second-order conditioning occurs when a new neutral stimulus is paired with an already-conditioned stimulus, becoming conditioned itself.
40.
A schedule of reinforcement that produces the highest rate of responding and is most resistant to extinction is:
C. Variable-ratio schedules (e.g., gambling) produce high, steady response rates and strong resistance to extinction because the next reinforcement could always be imminent.
41.
In Tulving's distinction, remembering that you had eggs for breakfast is an example of:
C. Episodic memory concerns personally experienced events bound to time and place. Semantic memory contains general world knowledge.
42.
The 'tip-of-the-tongue' phenomenon most clearly demonstrates:
B. TOT states illustrate that memory retrieval can be partial — one may access phonological fragments, first letters, or syllable counts without full lexical access.
43.
Mental rotation experiments by Shepard and Metzler demonstrated that:
B. Shepard & Metzler (1971) showed that time to decide if two 3D shapes are identical increases linearly with angular disparity, supporting analog (depictive) representation of imagery.
44.
The 'garden-path' sentence illustrates:
B. Garden-path sentences (e.g., 'The horse raced past the barn fell') show readers commit to a parse early and must reanalyze when subsequent words reveal the initial parse was wrong.
45.
Which finding best supports the levels-of-processing framework?
B. Craik & Lockhart argued memory depends on depth, not duration, of processing. Semantic encoding consistently yields better retention than phonological or structural encoding.
46.
Which of the following is an example of functional fixedness?
A. Functional fixedness is the tendency to see objects only in their customary use, impairing creative problem-solving. Duncker's candle problem and Maier's two-string problem illustrate it.
47.
The serial position effect produces:
B. Primacy effects reflect long-term storage from rehearsal; recency effects reflect short-term store; middle items suffer both decay and interference.
48.
Which of the following is a top-down influence on perception?
C. Top-down processing uses expectations, prior knowledge, and context. Recognizing an ambiguous letter faster in a word context exemplifies this.
49.
Implicit memory is most commonly assessed using:
D. Implicit memory reveals itself in performance changes without conscious recollection, typically measured via priming paradigms such as word-stem or fragment completion.
50.
According to signal detection theory, a 'hit' is:
B. SDT distinguishes hits, misses, false alarms, and correct rejections. Response bias (β) and sensitivity (d′) can be separately estimated from these outcomes.
51.
Spreading activation in semantic networks predicts that:
B. Collins & Loftus's spreading activation theory predicts that activating one node (e.g., BREAD) facilitates retrieval of associated nodes (BUTTER), producing semantic priming effects.
52.
The 'cocktail party effect' — noticing your name in an unattended conversation — is evidence for:
B. The effect supports Treisman's attenuation model and late-selection theories: unattended information is processed at least somewhat, and salient material can break through.
53.
A concept-driven expectation that determines how ambiguous information is interpreted is:
A. Schemas are organized knowledge structures that guide attention, encoding, and interpretation of new information, often leading to assimilation of ambiguous material into existing patterns.
54.
Research on bilingualism generally shows that bilinguals:
A. Bilinguals typically show slightly smaller vocabularies in each language individually but advantages on tasks requiring inhibitory control and task switching.
55.
Which problem-solving approach guarantees a solution if one exists?
B. An algorithm is a step-by-step procedure guaranteed to produce a solution, though possibly inefficient. Heuristics are faster shortcuts that do not guarantee solutions.
56.
In classical conditioning terminology, a previously neutral stimulus that has come to elicit a response through pairing is the:
C. After pairing with a US, the neutral stimulus becomes a conditioned stimulus capable of eliciting a conditioned response.
57.
Thorndike's Law of Effect states that behaviors followed by satisfying consequences:
B. Thorndike's Law of Effect, derived from his puzzle-box experiments, laid the groundwork for Skinner's operant conditioning: behavior is shaped by its consequences.
58.
A child who sees a hamster and calls it 'doggie' is displaying:
B. Assimilation incorporates new information into existing schemas ('four-legged furry animal = doggie'). Accommodation modifies schemas to fit new information.
59.
Tolman's demonstration of latent learning in rats was important because it showed:
B. Tolman's rats explored mazes without reinforcement but demonstrated learning once reinforcement was introduced — evidence for cognitive maps and latent learning, challenging strict behaviorism.

Social Psychology

§ III
60.
In Milgram's obedience studies, what percentage delivered the maximum 450-volt shock in the baseline condition?
B. About 65% (26 of 40) of Milgram's initial participants administered the full 450 volts, demonstrating the power of legitimate authority.
61.
Attributing others' behavior to disposition while attributing one's own to situation is called:
D. Actor-observer bias contrasts attributions for self vs. others. The fundamental attribution error refers specifically to overattributing others' behavior to disposition.
62.
Festinger's theory of cognitive dissonance predicts attitude change is GREATEST when:
A. Festinger & Carlsmith's $1 vs. $20 study showed participants paid $1 to lie about a boring task liked it more — insufficient external justification forces internal change.
63.
The bystander effect is best explained by which combined mechanisms?
C. Darley & Latané identified these as core mechanisms: responsibility is spread across bystanders, and each reads others' inaction as evidence no emergency exists.
64.
Sherif's Robbers Cave study showed intergroup hostility is most effectively reduced through:
E. Mere contact did not reduce conflict between the Rattlers and Eagles. Only superordinate goals (e.g., fixing the water supply) successfully reduced hostility.
65.
Which was NOT identified by Asch as affecting conformity rates?
B. Cognitive load was not a variable in Asch's line-judgment paradigm. A single dissenter dramatically reduced conformity.
66.
The mere exposure effect refers to:
B. Zajonc demonstrated that repeated exposure to a neutral stimulus typically increases liking, even when exposures are subliminal.
67.
Self-serving bias involves attributing:
B. The self-serving bias protects self-esteem by crediting the self for successes and blaming the situation for failures — the opposite of the pattern seen in depression.
68.
The elaboration likelihood model distinguishes between:
B. Petty & Cacioppo's ELM: the central route involves careful argument scrutiny under high motivation/ability; the peripheral route relies on cues (attractiveness, source expertise) under low elaboration.
69.
Deindividuation is most likely to occur when:
B. Deindividuation involves loss of self-awareness and restraint under conditions of anonymity, arousal, and group immersion, producing behavior inconsistent with personal norms.
70.
Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment is most often cited as evidence for:
B. Though methodologically controversial, the study is cited for its dramatic demonstration of how assigned roles (guard vs. prisoner) rapidly shape behavior, though recent scholarship has questioned its conclusions.
71.
Groupthink, as described by Janis, is characterized by:
B. Groupthink describes deterioration of decision-making in cohesive groups under stress: pressure toward unanimity, self-censorship, illusion of invulnerability, and stereotyping of outgroups.
72.
The 'foot-in-the-door' technique involves:
B. Foot-in-the-door exploits commitment and consistency: after complying with a small request, people are more likely to comply with a larger related one. 'Door-in-the-face' reverses the pattern.
73.
Social facilitation predicts that the presence of others:
B. Zajonc's model: the presence of others increases arousal, which strengthens the dominant response. On well-learned/simple tasks this helps; on novel/complex tasks it hurts.
74.
According to balance theory (Heider), tension in social triads is produced by:
B. Heider's balance theory: a triad is balanced if the product of signs is positive. An odd number of negative relations produces imbalance, motivating attitude change to restore balance.
75.
Contact between groups is most likely to reduce prejudice when it includes all of the following EXCEPT:
E. Allport's classic contact hypothesis specifies equal status, shared goals, cooperation, and institutional support. Anonymity of group membership is not a condition; meaningful contact requires awareness of the category.
76.
The 'ultimate attribution error' refers to:
B. Pettigrew extended the FAE to the intergroup level: negative outgroup behavior is attributed to dispositions, positive outgroup behavior to situations — the opposite pattern for ingroup behavior.
77.
Which experimental finding supports minority influence in groups?
B. Moscovici's research showed that a consistent, confident minority can produce genuine attitude change in the majority, often via private conversion even when public conformity is low.
78.
Reactance theory predicts that when personal freedom is threatened, individuals will:
B. Brehm's reactance theory: threats to autonomy produce motivational arousal to reassert the threatened freedom, often by increased desire for the forbidden option.

Developmental Psychology

§ IV
79.
According to Piaget, a child who understands that liquid amount is unchanged when poured into a different glass has achieved:
C. Conservation emerges in the concrete operational stage (7–11). Before this, children are fooled by perceptual changes like the taller column.
80.
In Ainsworth's Strange Situation, an infant who shows little distress at separation and avoids the caregiver at reunion is classified as:
A. The four attachment classifications are secure, insecure-avoidant, insecure-resistant/ambivalent, and disorganized. Avoidant infants show minimal distress and active avoidance at reunion.
81.
Erikson's fifth psychosocial stage, in adolescence, involves the conflict between:
D. Erikson's eight stages: trust/mistrust, autonomy/shame, initiative/guilt, industry/inferiority, identity/role confusion (adolescence), intimacy/isolation, generativity/stagnation, integrity/despair.
82.
Vygotsky's 'zone of proximal development' refers to:
B. The ZPD is the gap between actual and potential performance. Instruction targeted to this zone, with scaffolding, is considered maximally effective.
83.
Kohlberg's postconventional level of moral reasoning is characterized by:
E. Postconventional (Stages 5–6) involves social contract and universal principles. C is Stage 4 (law-and-order); B is Stage 3 (good-boy/nice-girl).
84.
Which is most characteristic of Baumrind's authoritative parenting style?
A. Authoritative combines high control with warmth. Authoritarian = high control/low warmth; permissive = low/high; uninvolved = low/low.
85.
Harlow's infant rhesus monkey studies demonstrated that:
C. Infant monkeys clung to a cloth 'mother' for comfort even when a wire 'mother' provided milk, directly challenging drive-reduction accounts of attachment.
86.
A child who understands that others can hold beliefs different from her own has developed:
C. Theory of mind — the understanding that others have mental states that may differ from one's own — typically emerges around age 4, demonstrated by false-belief tasks.
87.
The 'visual cliff' apparatus developed by Gibson and Walk assessed:
B. Infants old enough to crawl typically refuse to cross the apparent drop-off on a glass surface, indicating functional depth perception.
88.
Which trimester is typically associated with the greatest sensitivity to teratogens affecting organogenesis?
A. Major organ systems form during the embryonic period (weeks 3–8), making the first trimester the period of greatest vulnerability to structural teratogenic effects.
89.
Babbling in infants typically begins at approximately what age?
C. Canonical (reduplicated) babbling — 'bababa' — emerges around 6 months. First true words appear around 10–14 months; two-word utterances around 18–24 months.
90.
Piaget's sensorimotor stage culminates in the development of:
C. The sensorimotor stage (birth–2 years) ends with fully developed object permanence — the understanding that objects continue to exist when out of sight.
91.
Gilligan's critique of Kohlberg's moral development theory argued that Kohlberg's scheme:
B. Gilligan argued that Kohlberg's justice-oriented framework systematically scored care/relationship-based reasoning (common in her female samples) as less mature.
92.
Which reflex disappears in infants around 3–4 months of age?
A. The Moro reflex and other primitive reflexes (rooting, palmar grasp, Babinski) diminish as cortical inhibition develops. Patellar, blink, pupillary, and gag persist through life.
93.
According to Marcia's theory of identity development, an adolescent who has made commitments without exploring alternatives is in:
C. Marcia's four statuses cross exploration and commitment: foreclosure = commitment without exploration; moratorium = exploring without commitment; achievement = both; diffusion = neither.
94.
Crystallized intelligence is most likely to:
B. In Horn & Cattell's framework, crystallized intelligence (knowledge, vocabulary, cultural facts) generally rises through adulthood, while fluid intelligence (novel problem-solving) declines earlier.
95.
A toddler who can complete a puzzle only with the aid of an adult is operating in his:
B. The ZPD is Vygotsky's region between current independent performance and potential performance with scaffolding — the space where effective instruction occurs.
96.
The infant characteristic of 'slow to warm up' in Thomas and Chess's temperament typology describes a child who is:
B. Thomas & Chess identified three temperamental clusters: easy (~40%), difficult (~10%), and slow-to-warm-up (~15%), with the remainder mixed. Slow-to-warm children show mild, initially wary reactions that soften over time.

Clinical & Abnormal Psychology

§ V
97.
Per DSM-5, a diagnosis of major depressive disorder requires symptoms for a minimum of:
B. MDD requires at least five symptoms (including depressed mood or anhedonia) during the same 2-week period. Persistent depressive disorder (dysthymia) requires 2 years.
98.
The dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia is supported by evidence that:
D. Traditional antipsychotics block D2 receptors, reducing positive symptoms. Conversely, dopamine agonists like amphetamines can induce or worsen psychotic symptoms.
99.
Beck's cognitive triad of depression consists of negative views about:
A. Beck's cognitive triad is the cornerstone of cognitive therapy for depression: negative schemas about oneself, the world, and the future.
100.
Systematic desensitization, developed by Wolpe, is based on the principle of:
C. The client is taught relaxation, then progressively exposed to anxiety-eliciting stimuli while maintaining the incompatible relaxation response.
101.
Which personality disorder is characterized by pervasive disregard for others' rights, deceit, and lack of remorse?
B. Antisocial personality disorder (Cluster B) includes deceitfulness, impulsivity, aggressiveness, recklessness, irresponsibility, and lack of remorse, with evidence of conduct disorder before age 15.
102.
The technique of saying whatever comes to mind without censorship is called:
E. A core psychoanalytic technique developed by Freud, free association is thought to bypass conscious defenses. Unconditional positive regard is Rogerian.
103.
Recurrent panic attacks followed by avoidance of situations where escape might be difficult best describes:
A. Agoraphobia commonly co-occurs with panic disorder as a conditioned avoidance response. In DSM-5, agoraphobia can also stand alone.
104.
In Rogers' person-centered therapy, the three essential therapist conditions are:
D. Rogers' 'necessary and sufficient' conditions place the therapeutic relationship, not technique, at the center of change.
105.
Tardive dyskinesia is a potential long-term side effect of:
C. Chronic D2 blockade from typical (first-generation) antipsychotics can produce tardive dyskinesia — involuntary, often irreversible movements of face and limbs. Atypical agents have lower but nonzero risk.
106.
A defining symptom of obsessive-compulsive disorder is:
B. OCD involves obsessions (intrusive, unwanted thoughts) and/or compulsions (repetitive ritualized behaviors) that the person typically recognizes as excessive or unreasonable.
107.
PTSD can be distinguished from acute stress disorder primarily by:
B. Per DSM-5, acute stress disorder is diagnosed 3 days to 1 month after trauma. PTSD requires symptoms lasting more than 1 month.
108.
Dissociative identity disorder is most strongly associated with:
B. DID is most commonly linked to severe, repeated early childhood abuse, consistent with dissociation as a maladaptive coping response to overwhelming trauma.
109.
Ellis's rational-emotive behavior therapy focuses primarily on:
B. REBT's ABC model: Activating event → irrational Belief → emotional/behavioral Consequence. Therapy targets disputing the beliefs to change consequences.
110.
Anorexia nervosa is distinguished from bulimia nervosa primarily by:
B. DSM-5 anorexia nervosa requires significantly low weight relative to expectation, whereas bulimia nervosa typically occurs at normal or above-normal weight despite binge-purge cycles.
111.
The most prevalent lifetime mental disorder in the United States is generally reported to be:
C. Anxiety disorders collectively (generalized anxiety, phobias, panic, social anxiety) have the highest lifetime prevalence among DSM disorders in large U.S. epidemiological surveys (~30%).
112.
Bipolar I disorder is distinguished from bipolar II by:
A. Bipolar I requires at least one manic episode (±depression). Bipolar II requires hypomania + at least one major depressive episode, with no full manic episode.
113.
Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) are first-line treatment for which of the following?
C. SSRIs (fluoxetine, sertraline, escitalopram) are first-line for MDD and are also commonly used for OCD, panic disorder, PTSD, and generalized anxiety.
114.
A client repeatedly projects feelings about a parent onto the therapist. A psychodynamic therapist would call this:
B. Transference is the client's displacement of feelings about significant others onto the therapist. Countertransference is the reverse — the therapist's feelings toward the client.
115.
The most robust biological evidence for schizophrenia as at least partly heritable comes from:
B. MZ twin concordance (~48%) is substantially higher than DZ concordance (~17%), pointing to genetic contribution while leaving room for environmental factors.
116.
Which pharmacologic class is first-line for acute mania in bipolar disorder?
C. Lithium and anticonvulsant mood stabilizers (valproate, lamotrigine) are first-line for bipolar mania. Antidepressant monotherapy risks precipitating mania.
117.
Dialectical behavior therapy was developed primarily to treat:
B. Marsha Linehan developed DBT specifically for BPD, integrating cognitive-behavioral techniques with mindfulness and dialectical strategies of acceptance and change.
118.
A 'positive symptom' of schizophrenia refers to:
B. Positive symptoms are additions to normal experience (hallucinations, delusions, disorganized speech). Negative symptoms are losses (flat affect, avolition, alogia).
119.
Exposure and response prevention (ERP) is the gold-standard behavioral treatment for:
B. ERP deliberately exposes the client to obsession-triggering stimuli while preventing the compulsive response, allowing extinction of the anxiety–ritual association.

Measurement, Methodology & History

§ VI
120.
A Type I error occurs when a researcher:
B. Type I (α) = false positive; Type II (β) = false negative. Power = 1 − β.
121.
A correlation coefficient of r = −0.85 indicates:
C. Magnitude indicates strength; sign indicates direction. Correlation does not imply causation regardless of magnitude.
122.
Which measure of central tendency is most influenced by extreme outliers?
A. The mean incorporates every value; outliers pull it disproportionately. Median and mode are robust to outliers. Range and IQR measure dispersion.
123.
A test producing similar scores across administrations demonstrates:
D. Test-retest reliability concerns temporal stability. Internal consistency (Cronbach's α) concerns item agreement; validity concerns whether the test measures what it claims.
124.
A double-blind study is one in which:
B. Double-blinding protects against participant expectancy (placebo) and experimenter effects (bias in interaction or scoring).
125.
The WAIS produces scores standardized to a mean of 100 and a standard deviation of:
C. WAIS and modern Stanford-Binet use M = 100, SD = 15. A score of 130 ≈ +2 SD (~98th percentile).
126.
The school founded by J.B. Watson that emphasized observable behavior and rejected introspection was:
A. Watson's 1913 manifesto launched behaviorism. Structuralism (Titchener) and functionalism (James) relied heavily on introspection.
127.
ANOVA is most appropriate to compare:
E. ANOVA tests whether at least one of three or more group means differs. t-tests compare two means; chi-square compares frequencies; Pearson's r assesses correlation.
128.
A scale with equal intervals but no true zero is:
D. Stevens' hierarchy: nominal → ordinal → interval (equal intervals, arbitrary zero) → ratio (equal intervals, true zero). Celsius is interval; Kelvin is ratio.
129.
William James is most closely associated with:
B. James's 1890 Principles of Psychology emphasized adaptive mental function, influenced by Darwin. Titchener represented the rival structuralist school.
130.
Counterbalancing is useful for controlling:
C. Within-subjects designs risk fatigue, practice, or carryover effects. Counterbalancing varies order so these effects average out.
131.
The APA requirement to inform participants about the true nature of a study after deception is called:
A. Debriefing occurs after participation to explain deception, address distress, and clarify purpose. Informed consent precedes participation.
132.
Wilhelm Wundt is generally credited with:
B. Wundt's 1879 Leipzig laboratory is the conventional origin of experimental psychology as a formal discipline.
133.
Statistical power is defined as:
B. Power = 1 − β = probability of detecting an effect if one exists. It increases with sample size, effect size, and alpha level.
134.
A sample is considered 'representative' if it:
A. Random sampling is the standard procedure for generating representative samples. Volunteer samples, matched samples, and convenience samples can bias estimates.
135.
Which of the following is an example of a quasi-experimental design?
B. Quasi-experiments compare groups that were not randomly assigned. Because confounds cannot be fully controlled, causal inferences are weaker than in true experiments.
136.
The standard error of the mean decreases as:
B. SE = s/√n. Increasing n shrinks the SE, yielding more precise estimates of the population mean.
137.
A normal distribution has what percentage of scores within ±1 standard deviation of the mean?
C. Approximately 68% of scores fall within ±1 SD, 95% within ±2 SD, and 99.7% within ±3 SD in a normal distribution.
138.
Which scale of measurement only classifies objects without ordering them?
A. Nominal scales (e.g., gender, diagnosis) only identify categories. Ordinal scales add rank; interval adds equal spacing; ratio adds a true zero.
139.
Convergent validity is established when:
B. Convergent validity: the measure agrees with other indicators of the same construct. Discriminant validity: the measure does NOT correlate strongly with measures of distinct constructs.
140.
A pilot study is most useful for:
B. Pilot studies test materials, procedures, and logistics on a small scale to refine methodology before committing to the main investigation.
141.
Sigmund Freud's structural model of the psyche consists of:
B. Freud's structural model (1923 'The Ego and the Id') posits id (primary process), ego (reality principle), and superego (internalized morality). The first option describes his earlier topographic model.
142.
If a distribution is positively skewed, which relationship holds?
B. Positive skew: a long right tail pulls the mean rightward, so mean > median > mode. Negative skew reverses this.
143.
A z-score of +1.96 corresponds approximately to what percentile?
D. z = ±1.96 bounds the central 95% of a normal distribution, so +1.96 ≈ 97.5th percentile. This is the basis of 95% confidence intervals in z-based inference.
144.
Ebbinghaus's pioneering memory research is most closely associated with:
B. Ebbinghaus (1885), using nonsense syllables on himself, showed exponential-like forgetting over time — the 'forgetting curve' — and pioneered quantitative study of memory.
